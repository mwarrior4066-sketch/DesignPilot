## Input Prompt
Refine the positioning for a design-ops AI tool aimed at independent designers who distrust generic AI outputs but still want speed.

- Phase: strategy
- Skills: `brand-strategy-expert.md`, `content-and-case-study-expert.md`

## Final Output
### Audience and problem
The primary segment is independent designers and small studio operators who need faster help but do not trust generic AI to respect structure, implementation reality, or evidence burden. Their pain is not “lack of ideas.” It is low-trust acceleration.

### Positioning frame
Position the tool as a structured design operator, not as a creative co-pilot. The category frame matters because the obvious alternative is a broad AI assistant that promises everything and proves little. The wedge is disciplined routing, validation, and proof-aware output. This is also a category-convention constraint: the message has to feel trustworthy enough for working designers who already distrust vague AI claims.

### Trust and proof burden
The product cannot rely on adjectives like premium or thoughtful. It needs trust signals: visible route logic, worked examples, benchmark comparisons, and explicit handling of open proof gaps. Those trust signals are implementation constraints, not optional garnish, because the product promise breaks if the homepage, case-study lede, and onboarding copy all describe the system differently. The tradeoff is warmth versus credibility. Credibility must win first because the buyer’s real fear is sloppy output disguised as confidence.

### Messaging consequences
Use language that promises constrained help, not magic. Replace “get better design instantly” with “get routed, validated design help that shows its logic.” Keep tone direct. Avoid superiority claims unless a comparison artifact or measured outcome exists.


## Extra implementation note
- The homepage, case-study lede, and benchmark summary should all use the same category frame so the trust signal does not fragment across surfaces.
- The obvious alternative is to let each artifact choose its own tone, but that would make the brand feel broad instead of disciplined.

## Why This Passed
- It defines a real segment and pain instead of pretending the audience is “everyone using AI.”
- It names the category alternative and the real wedge, so the positioning can survive comparison.
- It translates strategy into language behavior, which makes the output usable for copy and case-study work.
- It includes the governing trust tradeoff and explains why credibility has to outrank decorative warmth at this stage.

## What Would Have Failed
- Generic adjectives with no decision consequence.
- Calling the brand “best-in-class” or “validated” with no proof artifact.
- Treating interface style as the positioning itself.
- Using tone words as a substitute for audience and trust logic.

## Revision Pass
The early draft leaned too hard on tone words like rigorous and elevated.
The revision pushed those down and rebuilt the strategy around the actual trust burden.
It also added the category alternative and the proof burden so the output could guide real messaging decisions instead of just sounding sharp.
